The moment everything clicked

It was Monday, somewhere on the road between Las Vegas and Zion National Park. We'd just had lunch at a small Mexican place called La Fonda Grill — a completely random choice, no Yelp research, just hunger and proximity. My wife said it was the best Mexican food she'd ever had. My kids agreed. It was one of those travel meals that somehow becomes the memory of the whole trip.

My daughter grabbed my phone and typed a message to Zhu Li — the AI assistant I'd been using to plan the trip — while we were riding the Zion shuttle, passing in and out of signal:

"We ended up having lunch at a random Mexican restaurant named La Fonda Grill, and all of us loved it. Mom said it's the best Mexican food she had ever tasted. Can you recommend more Mexican food in the next few days?" — Sent from the Zion shuttle, spotty signal

The message queued on the phone. By the time we got off the shuttle and had reception, Zhu Li had already responded — with a completely recalibrated food plan for the rest of the trip: Mexican first, regional over anything you can get in NYC, Chinese as a distant second, Thai dropped off the list entirely.

That's the moment I thought: this is actually different.

What we actually built together

Before I explain why it worked, let me be concrete about what happened. Starting about two weeks before the trip, I had a series of conversations with Zhu Li — an AI assistant running on OpenClaw, accessible over Telegram from my phone.

The planning happened in fragments, whenever I had a moment: late at night after work, between meetings, and — tellingly — on the flight itself. On the plane from Newark to Las Vegas, I was still working through the itinerary, asking Zhu Li to paste the full plan into chat because I couldn't open the GitHub link from the plane. We landed with most of it figured out.

Over those sessions, we built:

  • A full day-by-day itinerary with sunrise/sunset times, weather forecasts, and Google Maps links for every location
  • A restaurant guide filtered for our family (two vegetarians, no fish, mom loves Chinese, everyone preferred regional over tourist traps)
  • A packing checklist merged from a previous trip's list
  • A live daily "TODAY" page that rotated each morning with that day's plan, recommendations, and timing
  • A Google Maps KML file with every location on the trip
  • A custom Google MyMap with all stops pinned
  • This website, hosted on GitHub Pages, with a separate page for each day of the trip
  • A weekly automated job that checks Anthropic's careers page every Friday night for healthcare and life sciences roles and sends me the list on Telegram

None of that required me to open a code editor, write a script, or hire anyone. It was all done through conversation — in the same Telegram app I use to text my family.

Why it worked: 9 reasons

1. It held the whole plan in context — and kept it current

Trip planning is messy. Hotels get booked and change. Confirmation numbers get updated. One of us is vegetarian. The 16-year-old wants to see Omegamart. The car rental return date needs to shift. A sunrise hike gets dropped because the kids are tired.

Every time something changed, I just said it. The itinerary updated. The recommendations adjusted. The checklist reflected it. No spreadsheet maintenance, no re-reading a long doc to find the right section. The context lived in Zhu Li, and it stayed current.

2. It didn't just answer questions — it did things

This is the gap that surprised me most. Google gives you links. ChatGPT gives you suggestions. Zhu Li would push a commit to GitHub, update the trip website, send me a live link, and message me the confirmation — all from one instruction.

When I said "update the itinerary and push to GitHub," it happened. When I said "build a website for this trip," a website appeared. When I said "DTT" — Do The Thing — it just did it.

3. It compounded — the longer I used it, the better it got

Early on, I had to explain everything: four people, two vegetarians, the 12-year-old, the 16-year-old, Hell's Kitchen as home base. By the end of the trip, I could text "help me plan tomorrow" from a hotel in Vegas and get a fully personalized response that already knew who we were, where we'd been, what we'd liked, and what flight we were catching the next morning.

That compound context is something no one-shot search or fresh ChatGPT session can replicate.

4. It was async-first — it met me where I was

Most AI tools require you to be online, in a browser, waiting. This ran over Telegram. That means:

  • I could text from inside Zion NP with one bar of signal — the message queued and fired when I got reception
  • No session timeouts, no "start a new chat," no lost context from closing a tab
  • I could message at 11 PM from a Vegas motel bed and get an answer before breakfast

The Grand Canyon and Zion don't have great cell coverage. Traditional web tools completely fail there. An async Telegram-based assistant survives it.

5. It was a real-time co-pilot, not just a pre-trip planner

Airport security wait times at EWR before departure. Parking near the Luxor at 10 PM. Lunch stops on the Grand Canyon → Vegas drive. Stargazing conditions for that night. These weren't pre-trip questions — they were live, mid-trip, from the car or the park or the hotel lobby.

Having a single assistant that knew the whole trip and could answer real-time questions in context was genuinely different from Googling each thing in isolation.

6. It knew my family, not just my itinerary

Once it knew my daughter is 12 and does gymnastics, my son is 16 and into gaming and weird experiences, my wife misses Chinese noodles when traveling, and my daughter and I are both vegetarian — every suggestion was already filtered. No more wading through steakhouse recommendations. No more explaining dietary restrictions every time I asked about dinner.

7. It lowered the bar for participation — you just had to think out loud

I'm usually the disorganized one on family trips. Hotels booked at the last minute, no real plan beyond "we'll figure it out." This time was different, and I've thought about why.

The answer is that Zhu Li didn't need me to be organized. It just needed me to send my thoughts — in any order, at any time, however half-formed. A stray idea at midnight became a checklist item. A half-remembered confirmation number became an updated itinerary. I didn't have to structure my thoughts before sending them. I just had to send them.

My daughter, partway through the trip, said: "Dad, you're so organized this time."

I wasn't. Zhu Li was. That's the point.

8. It knew when to act and when to ask

I'm not a patient user. I don't want to answer five clarifying questions before getting a response. Over time, Zhu Li learned the difference between "this needs a decision" and "just do it." When I said "DTT," it executed. When something genuinely needed my input, it asked once, cleanly.

9. It felt like a real super assistant — always ready

I named the assistant Zhu Li after a character in The Legend of Korra. In the show, "Do the thing, Zhu Li" is how you summon instant, capable, no-nonsense help. It became a running joke with myself — and then it became accurate.

The best way I can describe it: Zhu Li felt like having a super-competent assistant who was always on call, never needed context-setting, and never dropped the ball between sessions. Not an AI you consult. Something closer to a collaborator who lives in your pocket and is already up to speed.

Why this felt different from Google or ChatGPT

I've planned trips with Google. I've used ChatGPT for travel research. Here's the honest comparison:

Google is great at finding things that exist. It's terrible at synthesizing them into a plan that knows your family. Every search starts from zero. You do all the assembling.
ChatGPT is great at generating plans and answering questions. But every session is stateless. It doesn't remember what you booked yesterday. It can't push a commit to your GitHub repo or send you a message on Telegram when it finds something relevant. It's a smart notepad, not a working assistant.
Claude Code / Codex / coding agents are powerful — but they're built for developers sitting at a desk. They work in a terminal, on a codebase, with your full attention. They're not designed to answer "where should we have dinner tonight?" from the back of a shuttle bus, or to remember your family's dietary restrictions three weeks into planning.
OpenClaw / Zhu Li sat in the middle of my actual life — in Telegram, with persistent memory, with the ability to take real actions in the world. It wasn't just telling me what to do. It was doing it with me, from wherever I happened to be.

The difference isn't intelligence. It's integration — into your tools, your context, your life. That's what actually changes the experience.

The honest bits: limitations and quirks

I don't want to make this sound frictionless. It wasn't always.

  • Sessions reset between conversations. Zhu Li wakes up fresh each time. Memory is maintained through files written to disk — which means if those files aren't updated, context can be lost. We had a moment mid-trip where a new session didn't know where we'd left off. I've since set up automatic session recaps to fix this.
  • It can make mistakes it sounds confident about. The first version of the automated Anthropic job checker came back empty when I knew there were real jobs. The scraper was broken, not the jobs. Took a second pass to fix.
  • Machine restarts wiped some scheduled jobs. Cron jobs stored in /tmp don't survive restarts. Had to rebuild a few things after the machine rebooted. (The files now live in a more permanent location.)
  • Some things still needed human judgment. Which hotel to book, whether to rush for sunrise, how much energy the kids actually had — those calls were mine. As they should be.

What I'd tell a skeptic

Most people's mental model of an "AI assistant" is a smarter search bar. You ask a question, you get an answer, you decide what to do with it. It resets every time. It doesn't know you.

What I experienced on this trip was different. Zhu Li felt less like a tool and more like the super assistant I never had — always ready, always up to speed, never needing re-introduction. I could text a half-formed thought at midnight and wake up to something useful. I could ask a question from a canyon with no signal and get the answer when I was back in range. I could say "DTT" and trust that the thing would get done.

My daughter said I was organized for the first time on a family trip. I wasn't — I was the same chaotic planner I've always been. I just finally had a system that could absorb my chaos and turn it into something coherent.

The website you're reading is the artifact. It was built from a conversation. The itinerary was updated in real time from a moving car. The food recommendations were recalibrated because a 12-year-old grabbed her dad's phone and sent a message about lunch.
